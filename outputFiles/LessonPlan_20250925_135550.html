HTML Workshop

Materials / Resources Needed

  • Computers with internet access
  • Projector or whiteboard
  • Markers or pens
  • Printed handouts with basic HTML examples
  • Pre-made HTML templates (optional, for students needing extra support)
  • Images for students to use in their webpages (e.g., downloaded pictures of animals, objects etc)

Instructional Steps / Procedure

  • Introduction/Warm-up (5 minutes): Start with a discussion about websites and what makes them interactive and engaging. Show examples of simple websites.
  • Teacher-Directed Activity (15 minutes): Introduce the concept of HTML as the building blocks of websites. Explain the basic structure of an HTML document: , , . Show examples on the projector. Introduce common tags:

  • Independent Practice (15 minutes): Students create their own simple webpage using headings, paragraphs, and an image. Provide clear instructions and encourage creativity.
  • Student-Directed Activity (10 minutes): Students share their webpages with a partner for peer review, focusing on correct tag usage and structure.
  • Closure (5 minutes): Review key concepts and answer any remaining questions. Discuss what they learned and how they can continue to learn more about HTML.

Assessment / Evaluation

  • Observation of student participation during class discussions and activities.
  • Review of student-created webpages for correct use of HTML tags and attributes.
  • Peer review feedback from student webpage sharing.

Guided Practice (10-15 minutes)

  • Teacher leads students through creating a simple webpage together, step-by-step. Focus will be on typing the correct code, understanding the placement of tags, and seeing the immediate visual results on their screens. Example: Create a webpage with a title "My First Webpage", a heading "Welcome!", a paragraph introducing themselves, and an image of their choice.

Closure (5 minutes)

  • Brief review of the day's learning. Ask students to summarize the main concepts: What is HTML? What are some important tags? What are attributes? What did they learn today that surprised them? End with positive reinforcement and encouragement for future exploration.

Introduction / Warm-Up (5-10 minutes)

  • Start by asking students what websites they like and why. Show examples of simple websites on the projector. Discuss the different elements on a webpage (pictures, text, headings). Lead into the idea that these elements are created using a code called HTML.

Independent Practice (10-15 minutes)

  • Students create their own simple webpages individually. They are given a list of requirements (e.g., include a heading, two paragraphs, and an image) and encouraged to personalize their webpages with their own content and images.

Differentiation

  • For students who need extra support: Provide pre-made HTML templates with fill-in-the-blank sections for key elements. Pair them with more advanced students for peer support. Offer simplified instructions and one-on-one assistance.
  • For students who are ready for a challenge: Encourage them to experiment with additional HTML tags and attributes (e.g., different font styles, colors, links). Challenge them to create a more complex webpage with multiple sections.

Teacher Directed Activities

  • Introducing the concept of HTML and its structure.
  • Demonstrating the use of common HTML tags and attributes through examples.
  • Guiding students through creating a simple webpage together.
  • Reviewing student work and providing feedback.

Student Directed Activities

  • Creating their own simple webpages individually.
  • Sharing their webpages with a partner for peer review.
  • Exploring additional HTML tags and attributes on their own.

Extension, Refinement, and Practice Activities

  • Research and use more advanced HTML tags (e.g., lists, tables, links).
  • Create a webpage with multiple sections and organized content.
  • Explore online resources and tutorials for more in-depth learning.

Use of Media & Technology

  • Computers with internet access for students to create and view webpages.
  • Projector or whiteboard to display examples and instructions.
  • Images for students to use in their webpages (pre-downloaded or sourced online with teacher approval).
  • Online code editors (optional, for added convenience).

Cooperative Groupings

  • Pair students for peer review of their webpages.
  • Consider group work for more complex projects or if additional support is needed.
